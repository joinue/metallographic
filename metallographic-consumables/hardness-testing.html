Hardness Testing

Our hardness testing consumables ensure precise, consistent results. Choose from indenter tips, calibration blocks, and accessories for Vickers, Brinell, and Rockwell tests—essential for accurate material analysis and quality control.

Key Tips for Hardness Testing

  • Select the Right Indenter – Match the indenter (Vickers, Brinell, Rockwell) to the material and test method for consistent results.
  • Calibrate Regularly – Use certified test blocks to ensure ongoing accuracy and compliance with quality standards.
  • Prep the Surface – Polish and clean specimens thoroughly to avoid distorted or inaccurate readings.
  • Secure the Sample – Use proper anvil plates and holders to stabilize the specimen during testing.

Introduction

Hardness testing provides valuable data that correlates with material properties such as tensile strength, wear resistance, and ductility. These measurements are essential for quality control, material verification, and the selection process in metallographic analysis.

Rockwell Hardness

The Rockwell Hardness Test (HR) measures the depth of penetration under a major load after a minor preload is applied. It's fast, non-destructive, and commonly used across a range of ferrous and non-ferrous materials. Various Rockwell scales (A, B, C, etc.) use different indenters and load combinations to suit soft polymers to hardened steels.

Typical Applications:

  • Process control of hardened steel components (e.g., gears, shafts)
  • Monitoring heat treatment consistency
  • Evaluating weld hardness in structural assemblies
  • Rapid production-line inspection of metallic parts

Brinell Hardness

The Brinell Hardness Number (BHN or HBW) is calculated from the diameter of the indentation made by a 10 mm hardened steel or tungsten carbide ball under a specified load, typically 500–3000 kgf. It is best suited for soft to medium-hard materials like castings and forgings with rough or uneven surfaces.

Typical Applications:

  • Testing large, unmachined components like steel plates and billets
  • Evaluating cast iron and aluminum alloys
  • Correlating hardness with tensile strength in soft metals

Vickers Hardness

The Vickers Hardness Test (HV) uses a square-based pyramidal diamond indenter to create an impression under a precise load, typically from 1 gf to 100 kgf. It is extremely versatile and applicable to almost all metals and surface finishes. Hardness is calculated by measuring the diagonals of the indentation.

Typical Applications:

  • Characterizing hardness across a wide range of materials
  • Microstructure-sensitive testing (e.g., coatings, diffusion zones)
  • Thin cross-sections and small precision parts

Microhardness

Microhardness testing is used to measure surface or phase-specific hardness on a microscopic scale. It typically applies loads ranging from 1 to 2000 gf using a Vickers or Knoop diamond indenter. Indentations are optically measured, either manually or through image analysis systems.


Knoop hardness (HK) is ideal for thin coatings and microstructures. The test uses an elongated rhomboidal indenter and measures the long diagonal to calculate projected area.



Vickers microhardness is used for small regions or phase-specific areas. Hardness (HV) is calculated by dividing the applied load by the surface area of the impression, derived from the diagonal measurements.
